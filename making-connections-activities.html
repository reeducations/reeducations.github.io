18. rning Activity 8: Make a Cultural Content Connections Project related e text you have read in Learning Task 1. Do this in your notebook. Making Cultural Content Connections Project of Community Icon:​


Answer:

Macli-ing Dulag

Explanation:

Macli-ing Dulag was a respected elder of the Butbut tribe in the mountain village of Bugnay in the Cordilleras. He was considered wise and brave by those in the community, and he served three terms as barrio captain of Bugnay. In 1974, the Marcos regime planned on installing a massive dam along the Chico River. The dam would have been a 1000-megawatt hydroelectric power plant funded by the World Bank. It was planned that the structure would cover 1,400 sq. kilometers of rice terraces, orchards, and local graveyards. The administration thought it would meet little resistance from the indigenous tribes, even if it would displace over 100,000 people from their homes.

 

The people found their voice in Macli-ing Dulag. Though he was not formally educated, Dulag found the right words to defend his people. To this day, Dulag is known for his words against the dam project:

 

“Such arrogance to say that you own the land when you are owned by it! How can you own that which outlives you? Only the people own the land because only the people live forever. “

Dulag and other Cordillera leaders solidified opposition against the dam through a series of tribal pacts. Recognizing the critical role that he played, government soldiers murdered Dulag in his own house, surrounding it in the night and showering it with bullets. However, though their leader had died, the opposition against the dam stood firm. News of Dulag’s death spread all over the country and even abroad. The World Bank withdrew its funding, and eventually, the government withdrew its plans for the dam on the Chico River. The indigenous tribes kept their homes.

His name is carved in the  Wall of Heroes who fought against the tyrannical regime. His heroic deeds of standing against the dictator will always be remembered whenever government elements try to press down indigenous people and steal their lands.

Explanation:

The continuation of this year's lockdown had people anxious about the impending problems that are rooted on the possible loss of livelihood and work as sources of income.  The restrictions given on people's mobility has resulted for many establishments to close that led to people losing work and income.  However, instead of resulting to desperation, people have found a new way to empower themselves that is because one person stood up to enliven the bayanihan spirit in everyone.  Ana Patricia "Patreng" Non, an entrepreneur and alumna of the University of the Philippines has sparked the bayanihan spirit by putting up a small cart outside of her house in Maginhawa Quezon City where people can get food and other things that can help them for the day and share their excess which can be used by another family.  This simple act of giving and sharing has encourage farmers and other entities to donate and has grown into what is called the Maginhawa Community Pantry.  This humble act of kindness has since become a movement as people from all over the Philippine have started to put up community pantries in their areas.  By May, there are over 200 hundred pantries set up, and the movement has encouraged people to put up community pantries as far as our neighbor, Timor Leste.  Patreng may have faced setbacks and criticism for the growing community pantry movement, but just as every good story, her efforts to encourage people to sustain living by helping one another is too good not to win.
